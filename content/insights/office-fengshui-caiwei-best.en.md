Here's how it went. A couple of days ago another DM landed in my inbox asking me what exactly you should place at the Wealth Position, and whether just parking a Pi Xiu there does the trick. I've been asked this so many times these past two years that I could recite the textbook answer in my sleep, but every time I sit down to give a real answer, three sentences never cover it. So today I'm going to sort the whole thing out properly.

The conclusion first: the Wealth Position isn't as mystical as people make it out to be, and finding it isn't as complicated as you'd think. The simplest way to locate the Ming Cai Wei (Visible Wealth Position) is to stand at the office door and look inward. The corner diagonally opposite the door is basically it. No Luo Pan (Feng Shui Compass) required, no BaZi (Four Pillars of Destiny) chart to consult. For an ordinary office, this method gets you 80 to 90 percent of the way there. The Xuan Kong Flying Star system is more refined, factoring in how the stars shift each year, but for most people, finding the Ming Cai Wei and keeping it clean matters far more than fussing over yearly micro-adjustments. I've seen too many people dive headfirst into annual flying stars while a stack of unopened delivery boxes sits under their desk for half a year. The priorities are backwards.

Back to specifics. Let me break it down point by point.

1. Don't rush to place things. Clear the corner first. This is something I've said many times: the biggest sin at the Wealth Position isn't the absence of a Pi Xiu, it's the pile of clutter. Delivery boxes, old files, dead chargers stacked up there make anything you set on top of them useless. My own study corner became a temporary storage zone for a while, and when I finally remembered and cleared it out, it wasn't like money started rolling in, but that visual cleanness genuinely lifted my mood every time I walked in. This is a bit like the broken windows effect that environmental psychology often mentions: once a spot starts getting messy, it snowballs. Conversely, clean it up first and your own working state tightens up along with it.

2. There should be a solid wall behind the Wealth Position, not a walkway or a doorway airflow. The logic is plain. If you sit there working with nothing behind you, or with people constantly walking past, your subconscious stays on low-level alert. You can't settle in, and you certainly can't cultivate anything you'd call a wealth-accumulating mindset. If your office layout gives you no choice and there's a glass partition or corridor behind you, the fallback is a tall plant or bookshelf as a screen, just for that psychological grounding.

3. What to actually put at the Wealth Position, and the common pitfalls with each option. Water-grown Lucky Bamboo or money trees look nice, but if office ventilation is poor and nobody tends them daily, they rot at the roots and yellow easily. A withered plant at the Wealth Position looks worse than nothing at all. I've seen this happen too many times. By comparison, dry decor like an Amethyst Geode is lower maintenance. The only caveat is not to buy the cheap dyed kind. Ask whether it's natural before you pay. A traditional Ju Bao Pen (Treasure Bowl) also works, but don't leave it literally empty. Symbolically drop in a few coins or small stones. An empty Treasure Bowl feels off, symbolically speaking.

4. The Wealth Position should be well-lit, even if it's just a small night light. The traditional saying is that a bright Wealth Position is a thriving one. I'd rather understand it from another angle: a corner that stays dim is one you don't even bother looking at, let alone maintaining. Light is the lowest-cost maintenance reminder there is.

5. A few clear taboos. Don't treat the Wealth Position as a trash corner. I said this above but it's worth repeating, because it happens all the time. Don't have a mirror facing the Wealth Position directly, creating a reflective clash. Feng Shui values stable energy, and reflective objects like mirrors make the whole corner feel unsettled. Also don't place swords, cacti, or anything with sharp edges and corners at the Wealth Position. The saying about sharp corners piercing the person is, at bottom, a reminder not to give yourself visual discomfort.

6. If you want to go a step further, you can match the color and material of your Wealth Position decor to the Five Elements (Wu Xing) preferences in your own BaZi. For instance, if your BaZi favors Water, decor in black or blue or with a flowing shape suits you. If it favors Wood, a green plant beats crystal. That topic deserves its own article. Writing this far, I know some of you are wondering whether any of this actually works, or whether it's pure psychological comfort. Honestly, I understand the doubt. I thought the same when I first started studying this field. But over time I came to see it this way: even setting aside the metaphysics, the practical function of this system is more like a maintenance standard for your work corner. It forces you to clean regularly, keep it lit, and mind what's behind you. When someone sits every day in a clean, stable, well-lit spot, their state shifts. That sense of control is real, and has nothing to do with whether the Wealth Position calculation is accurate. If anything, the Wealth Position framework gives many people a reason they're willing to keep tidying their environment.

While I was drafting this piece, I came across an old line: Feng Shui doesn't tune fate, it tunes the mind. At first it sounded like a platitude, but on reflection it fits. Whether or not you believe in this system is one thing. Whether you're willing to spend two minutes a day tidying the corner where you spend eight hours is worth doing on its own merits.
